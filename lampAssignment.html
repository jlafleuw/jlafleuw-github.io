Illuminating the World Around Me (Making a Lamp) PLUS Molding and Casting Part 1

This will contain all of my documentation for the Lamp Assignment. As of November 8th, however, the page shows my inspiration, planning, and experimentation process with patterns and other elements.
My first step was to order a table lamp base so that I could have options, as I experimented, to make either a hanging lamp or a table lamp (see photo above). Happily, the measurements for the lamps are similar.

No matter what design I go with, it will need to fit neatly and tightly around these measurements.

Then I went online for some inspiration:

I know most of these are beyond my skill level, but they gave me a place to start with experimentation in Rhino and Grasshopper.


In my early Rhino experiments, I played with polysurfaces, planes, and meshes to try to design things I had seen online, like mushroom tops, twisted abstracts, warped "tulip petals," and more:






I stumbled on a design on which I am now working. The concept is a hanging moon-and-stars, in which the spherical 'moon' has a hole in the top which attaches to a ring support which fits around the 4cm circumference hanging cone, using hooks or tabs.
The bottom of the 'moon' will have either hook openings for the stars to attach (several attachment points per star), or, if there is a way, attachment points for filaments from which the stars can suspend (though I do not know how to do that, or if it can be done).
The PLA filament for the hollow moon will be a translucent one, as I plan a blue bulb inside. I hope to master silk or HTPLA filament for the stars so they can be in shiny, rainbow colors. I know the stars would have to have high rafts or brims for printing.
I am not 100% decided on this concept.

The sphere for the moon will be around 8-10cm diameter (hollow but thicker for tolerance and stable attachment points). The stars will vary in size from 8cm in height to 4cm in height, depending on the best way to attach them.

Then I got meshier. While playing with spheres, I mushed one into a blobby half-sphere which might be easier for the moon (for hanging stars), but also reminded me of a jelly, so I took off the top (which didn't measure right and pulled my mesh) and widened the dome:


I like this idea, the jelly tentacles seem easier to attach and print than the stars, and would be less time-consuming, but with the shiny HTPLA or silk would be just as effective.

My last experiment at this time is a meshy base lamp in a bulbuous shape that I made using LunchBox, where I experimented with some online instructions from Cults on how to make a cover and attachment for the lamp base to go with it. I would still need to shrink the lower part of the bulb even more, I am working in that.

Part 2: The Lamp Adventure Continues!


While I worked for a while on the Jelly hanging lamp, I couldn't find a neat way, using holes or Boolean differences, to make the tentacles hang nicely, and time was starting to run short.

Seeing examples of Voronoi mesh in class made me really want to try making s Voronoi mesh bulb lampshade for my table lamp base, but first I had to figure out howe to make a strong Voronoi mesh in Grasshopper. This involved finding tutorial videos online and a lot of experimentation. I started with making a cone and a sphere.




Once I had a working sphere, I copied it into Rhino where I could put points into it and try to mold it into a shape. Later I had to make it taller to fit the bulb shaft and lightbulb, with room for space between the lampshade and bulb.



The Rest of the Story
A lampshade does not make a lamp. I still needed to design base elements to support and adhere the lampshade to my table lamp base. This would include a base casing for the lamp, a bulb shaft tube to cover the bulb shaft, and a top ring to shap into the base case and support the lampshade.

I found a tutorial and artist who helped me to get started with making these elements, as they had displayed their own similar design online, on Cults, linking to http://adafruit.com. Limor Fried is the artist who gave me some tips.

I was able to design these components in Rhino by making basic shapes, extruding them, and using careful measurements along with the actual lamp base measurements to get everything right (and remembering to leave things like a hole for the lamp cord, etc.).

Still, it took 6 tries to print the bulb shaft tube, and two tries each for the lamp base case and top ring, carefully aligning the Rhino specs in Cura. This ended up being the easy printing part.





It was time to start printing, and I began with the base components. The first base case was a spiralized version, for size.

Then I made adjustments (it was a little too big) and printed a real version, which fit perfectly. I moved on to the bulb shaft, which took 6 tries to get a perfect fit.





Finally, I moved on to printing the base ring, which only took 2 tries, and I was able to pause printing on the first try to test it with the base case, realised it was the wrong size, made adjustments, and reprinted.




The top ring and base case snap together, so the top ring can be a base for the lampshade. I will use Gorilla Glue to adhere the lampshade, since the top ring can unsnap from the base case (for bulb changes).


Now came the hardest part of this assignment - printing a Voronoi mesh. At first, I did not realize how many adjusted settings were necessary to print this style of mesh effectively. I had to stop a few prints very early when the printing of the mesh pulled the raft off the plate, causing nozzle clogs and wayward filament.
This prompted me to do research online. I found many articles and videos on printing Voronoi mesh, so this is not a problem specific to me. Adjustments of flow rates (very high), heat (nozzle and bed very hot), fan settings (very high), ahd other factors had to be considered.
Not only that, but adjustments vary by printer, and Creality printers, according to many websites, are the most difficult for printing Voronoi, and getting the settings right.
This was when I realized that I might not be able to actually print this lampshade, but I was determined to try. The hardest part is that the smallest version that would cover the lightbulb was a 17.5-hour print job at standard.
I made an emergency spherical mesh lampshade, just in case.


While each try got progressively better as I adjusted the settings, the time investment in the failed prints was frustrating (the first failed try was 5 hours in, the second was 7 hours in).

No matter the setting, the biggest problem was the nozzle/filament snagging, at some point, on a mesh tip, and pulling the entire print off the bed, wrecking the print. So I again increased and adjusted settings and then I broke out the big guns.


I had purchased this a while ago, but hadn't used it yet. Creality says it is the most adherent of all bed trays, and it is really sticky - if you use one, remember to have your hair pulled back.
I started printing again.

After 21 hours and many tries, my Voronoi mesh lampshade finished printing! It was rough, and needed a lot of sanding and trimming - it is still a little fuzzy.




Once I had done my best to remove the raft and sand and trim off the worst of the fuzz and stringy filament bits, I got ready to cure the Gorilla glue to adhere the lampshade to the top ring in my base setup. It took about an hour to cure.



Now it was time to try out my new lamp!

MOLDING AND CASTING PART 1: The Good Kind of Mold?

The instructions for this assignment confused me at first. Still, I went into Rhino and experimented with shapes, meshes, and Boolean differences until I made a mold for a small block.
At first, I was going to get fancy and make a little devil head, but I was having trouble with the dimensions, and it was taking too much time. I took Nadya's advice and went for the simple block shape instead.




This process took a lot of experimentation, so I kept extra copies of everything I had tried, in case I needed to reuse anything.




I also needed to print my object, and the halves of my object, the little block.

I used a simple cutting plane to split my block.

Once everything was drafted in Rhino, I set out to print.

My first mold was only kind of successful - I forgot to add a top layer.


Oops.

I went in to Cura and added a top layer and, after a brief interlude of replacing a clogged and damaged nozzle and fixing my extruder, I reprinted the mold.






I had to use sandpaper to get the edges (rims?) flush with each other after printing. I have a pouring spout, but because the mold is so small, I did not add a vent shaft. If I need one, I will use a drill.
